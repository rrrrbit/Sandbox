Substratology is the study of the structures and dynamics of consciousness-viable substrates, a generalisation of neuroscience. (See also: phenomenology as the study of subjective experiences, and substratophenomenology (colloquially spology) as the study of idiosyncratic correlations between subjective experience and substrate dynamics.)

Below is an outline of some substratological concepts.
# Substrate 
A consciousness-viable substrate is any system that can encode consciousness and subjective experience.
# Descriptive Concepts
## Structured Substrates
Structured substrates have some kind of inherent structure that affects the dynamics of the substrate and minds on it. Most biological substrates (e.g. the human brain) are structured substrates.
## Fluid Substrates
Fluid substrates are initially unstructured and homogeneous. Autotropic Liquid Crystal Substrate (ALCS) is a fluid substrate.
## Physical Substrates
Physical substrates are substrates that physically exist and whose dynamics are a direct consequence of physics.
## Abstract Substrates
Abstract substrates are non-physical (mathematical, computational, conceptual, etc.) substrates where dynamics are governed by abstract rules and relations over informational states. An abstract substrate may be physically instantiated but this does not equate to a physical substrate. Many neuromorphic systems count as abstract substrates. The hypothesis of the Noosphere as an abstract substrate is supported by some but subject to controversy.
## Artifical Substrates
Artificial substrates are deliberately designed. Substrates designed to host consciousness and those designed for computation that have accidentally given rise to consciousness are both considered artificial substrates.
## Biological Substrates
Biological substrates are those produced through evolution, without conscious interference. Brains are considered a biological substrate. The Eywa would be considered a biological substrate.
## Cultured Substrates
Cultured substrates are substrates artificially grown from biological tissue. Neural organoids are cultured substrates.
