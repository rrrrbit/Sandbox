(Hereafter, an associated quantity of a substrate is called a blob.)

A single blob does not necessarily correspond to a single individual. Many psychological plural experiences can be described through spology, but some may require different language as embodiment is no longer a given, and some concepts may be new altogether.
# Substrate fission    
When a single blob grows unstable, either from substratological trauma ([traumagenic](https://pluralpedia.org/w/Betagenic)) or size/cardinality (hypergenic), it may split apart into multiple more stable blobs. Substrate fission is possible in fluid substrates (most abstract substrates and some physical) but impossible, difficult or catastrophic in structured substrates (most biological substrates).  

These blobs may inherit aspects of the pre-split blob like memories, desires, goals and personality, in different ways, but are separate and stable. Hence, (full) substrate fission is generally not considered a plural experience in itself, but is closely related. If the multiple singlets share a body they may have similar experiences to pre-spological plurals, but can move to separate bodies fairly easily if desired.
# Partial fission
Fission is partial when blobs undergo fission but continue to remain connected to each other in some significant way, not separating fully. They may be dependent on each other or share spological structures or aspects like memory or mood, and may be able to merge again, but have other separated structures or aspects.

Partial fission is sometimes considered [a kind of plurality](https://pluralpedia.org/w/Blurian), depending on structure, which aspects are separated, and to what extent. It may be an involuntary or a [deliberate, highly trained](https://pluralpedia.org/w/Tulpamancy) outcome of fission.

Despite being quite risky, partial fission can allow a system to scale beyond pre-spological notions of [complurality](https://pluralpedia.org/w/Complural). It has [various uses](https://pluralpedia.org/w/Function_Fragment) by high-cardinality individuals:
- Networked awareness for overseeing very complex systems, such as in governance, business, city infrastructure or research simulations.
- Memory compartmentalisation for mass archivism.
# Multistability    
Multistability is when a single blob has multiple distinguishable stable states. A stable state may contain a [single active pattern](https://pluralpedia.org/w/Headmate), or [multiple superimposed](https://pluralpedia.org/w/Co-conscious). Stable states may have stable [substates](https://pluralpedia.org/w/Sisasystem). Patterns may [overlap or interfere](https://pluralpedia.org/w/Blending) in complex ways. Multistability is possible in structured substrates but more commonly seen in fluid substrates. An alter being active/conscious must not be confused with fronting, which is not relevant in the absence of a body.

A helpful metaphor is to picture the state of a blob as a ball on a bumpy surface. The ball can settle into pits on the surface, representing stable states. There may exist only a single pit, or multiple. Pits can exist inside other pits, or partially overlap. The bumpy surface may fluctuate over time, resulting in pits shallowing, deepening, merging or splitting.

Alters could hypothetically become cleanly separate blobs if the blob assimilates enough substrate to undergo hypergenic fission. However, the outcome is extremely difficult to control and it has never been recorded.
