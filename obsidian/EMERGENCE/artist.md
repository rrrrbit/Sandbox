Here's a story about An Artist. 

There was this guy maybe 20 or 30 years back. Neuroscientist turned artist. Very experimental, avant-garde kind of stuff. They did interviews with people he knew and they all said he was seemed reasonably well adjusted, a bit cryptic sometimes, but overall a cool guy and very clever. Though his art never really took off in his lifetime.

There is a clip that circulates, of how he died. The details are vague about whether it was live streamed, leaked, or faked. I haven't seen the clip myself. Apparently, he was hooked up to this computer. Pressed a button and just... keeled over. Seized up, started frothing at the mouth. Blood was dribbling out of his nose. Really morbid.

The story goes he kept a journal. Wasn't as well adjusted as everyone thought. Between scientific jargon and psychotic ramblings, this person's mind was evidently not peaceful. But the point is he was haunted by the idea that nobody would ever understand him. That he would never make enough art or have published enough papers for any person to ever _understand_ him. He wasnt exactly sane, but he really did love everyone and everything around him. And it broke his heart to think that he would never be able to share _everything_ he knew and felt with them. Not unless he ripped the thoughts out of his own head.

A few days after he died, a USB showed up at a former colleague's door. 

Data. Data of unprecedented detail. Terabytes and terabytes of it (which was a lot back then). It showed his brain activity in his last few minutes, as he died. Along with the data, writing about the hardware he used to record it and how it would be intepreted. And, in some versions of the story, the clip.

The data is exceptionally coherent, even by today's standards, for someone whose cerebrum was being shredded by some quite rudimentary and destructive electrodes, the best of his time for his purposes, but still. Some say they could even match up the song playing in the clip with what he was hearing as he went. It actually took a few years for anyone to produce a map that could match its level of coherence, even of a living subject. He did a great job holding on.

The public probably won't ever know what he was thinking as he died. As for me, I like to think the people that he loved and cared about got to see what he wanted to show them. But the bottom line is, what he gave us advanced neuroscience and neuromorphic computing by leaps and bounds. Enough to give birth to the field of substratology. 

Some even argue that he is the reason emergents can exist at all. And that's going just fine, ain't it?
